Challenges

30-Day Challenges

I like 30-day challenges because one month is long enough to matter and short enough to not feel like a prison sentence. Daily check-ins keep me honest.

I also built Challengeboard so this process is less spreadsheet and more usable: view the project write-up or open Challengeboard ↗.

Why I keep doing these

Personal reset

Thirty days is enough to shake loose a rut without turning life into a boot camp. Repeating one small action daily changes what feels normal.

Concrete progress

Vague goals become tiny daily actions with a finish line. That makes it easier to keep promises to yourself and easier to see what is actually working.

Better people habits

Doing challenges with other people is sneakily effective. Shared effort creates trust and a lot more follow-through than solo good intentions.

Completed challenges

Real examples, with enough detail to steal shamelessly.

  • Daily mobility: 15 minutes every morning for 30 days to feel less creaky.
  • Deep focus block: 90 distraction-free minutes each day on one meaningful project.
  • Connection challenge: One thoughtful call or message each day to a friend or family member.
  • Reading streak: 25 pages a day to finally shrink an intimidating nonfiction pile.

Challenge ideas (need co-conspirators)

If one of these sounds fun, reach out and we can do it together.

  • No-sugar month: 30 days with no added sugar, plus weekly check-ins and recipe swaps.
  • Share your work openly: Post one small public update daily on a project, essay, or prototype.
  • Daily creative reps: 20 minutes of writing, sketching, or music every day for 30 days.
  • Evening reset: Phone off by 9:00 PM plus a short nightly shutdown routine.
